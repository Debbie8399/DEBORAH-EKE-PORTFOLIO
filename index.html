Logistics Operations Analysis using Power BI

An end-to-end analysis of a US trucking operation — from raw data to interactive dashboard

Interact with the dashboard: View on Power BI

Main dashboard overview showing key operational KPIs

Introduction

This study analyzes three years of operational data from a US trucking company to understand where the business is making money, where it is losing money, and where service quality is breaking down. The trucking industry runs on thin margins, and most operators generate enormous amounts of transactional data that never gets connected into a single picture. This project set out to change that.

The dataset is made up of 14 related tables covering every part of the operation spanning 2022 to 2024 with roughly 550,000 rows of operational records.

Data Sources

Dimension tables (the "who, what, and where"):

  • customers — customer accounts, types, and contract details
  • drivers — driver profiles, experience, and home terminals
  • trucks — fleet equipment, make, model year, and status
  • trailers — trailer inventory, type, and length
  • routes — origin-destination lanes and typical distances
  • facilities — terminals and warehouses with locations and dock information

Fact tables (the "what happened"):

  • loads — every customer order, with revenue, weight, and route
  • trips — the actual movement of each load, with miles and fuel data
  • fuel_purchases — every fuel transaction across the fleet
  • maintenance_records — service and repair events per truck
  • delivery_events — pickup and delivery scans with detention and on-time flags
  • safety_incidents — accidents and violations with cost and fault details

Aggregate tables (pre-summarized monthly metrics): driver_monthly_metrics and truck_utilization_metrics

Data Cleaning and Preparation

The main tool used for this project is Power BI. Before any analysis could begin, the 14 raw tables needed to be cleaned and prepared in Power Query. This involved several steps:

1. Removed duplicate records. Each table was checked for duplicate rows, and primary key columns were checked to confirm every identifier was unique. Removing duplicates ensures that counts and totals are not inflated.

2. Fixed data types. Each column was assigned the proper type manually — ID columns set to text, date columns set to date, revenue and cost columns set to decimal numbers, and quantity columns set to whole numbers. Correct data types are essential because they control how relationships and calculations behave later.

3. Standardized text columns. Text fields such as city names, statuses, and categories were trimmed of extra spaces and standardized in casing so that values would match consistently across tables.

4. Handled missing values. Columns were reviewed for blanks. Where a blank carried meaning the value was left as is. Where a blank should be treated as zero, it was filled with zero so calculations would work correctly.

5. Created relationships between the tables. Once the tables were clean, relationships were built in the Model view to connect them. All relationships were set as one-to-many with a single filter direction, forming a clean star schema. A central Date table was created and connected to every table that has a date.

Data Visualization

The Overview page was designed to answer the most important questions at a glance, then let the user drill deeper. Each visual was chosen deliberately for the type of information it communicates.

KPI cards (Total Revenue, Total Profit, Total Miles, AVG MPG, Profit Margin %). Cards were used for the headline numbers because a single large figure is the fastest way to communicate a key metric. These five cards give an immediate summary of the entire operation's scale and health before the user examines any chart.

Top 10 Customers by Revenue — horizontal bar chart. A horizontal bar chart works well for ranking because it orders the items clearly and leaves enough room for long customer names to remain readable. It immediately shows which customers matter most and how concentrated the revenue is among the leading accounts.

Customer Type by Revenue — donut chart. A donut chart is effective for showing parts of a whole when there are only a few categories, which makes the overall revenue mix easy to understand at a glance.

Total Incident by Months — line chart. A line chart is the clearest way to show a trend across time, and plotting incidents month by month reveals whether safety events are rising, falling, or following a seasonal pattern across the year.

Fleet Utilization Rate — gauge. A gauge is ideal for a metric that should be read against its full potential, since it answers the question of how close the fleet is to being fully used. At eighty-three percent, the gauge shows at once that the fleet is being used efficiently.

AVG Revenue per Load — gauge. A second gauge displays the average value of each load, giving a quick read on how healthy the pricing is on each individual order.

Vehicle Damage Cost by Incident Type — column chart. A column chart makes it easy to compare values side by side, so the most expensive incident types, such as equipment damage and accidents, stand out clearly.

Monthly summary table (Revenue, Profit, Fuel Cost, Maintenance Cost, Miles). A table is the best choice when exact figures matter and when several related numbers need to be compared row by row, so it serves the users who want precise values rather than visual approximations.

AVG MPG by Months — line chart. A line chart shows how fuel efficiency changed across the year and helps identify any months where mileage dropped.

Slicers (Year, Quarter). Slicers were placed at the top of the page so that the entire dashboard can be filtered by time period. Because every table is connected to the central date table, selecting a year or a quarter updates every visual on the page at the same time.

Insights

1. On-time delivery is the biggest problem. Only about 44.6% of deliveries arrived on time, far below the industry standard of 90% or more. With more than half of all deliveries late, this is the single most important issue revealed by the data.

2. The lateness is not caused by a driver shortage. Pickups were on time about 67% of the time, but deliveries dropped to 45%. This gap suggests that delays build up during the trip — most likely from detention at customer facilities — rather than from a lack of drivers.

3. The business is concentrated in a few customers and routes. The top ten customers generated more than $50 million in revenue, and the single most profitable route produced $3.9 million in profit on its own.

4. A large share of safety incidents were preventable. About 37.6% of incidents were preventable, costing roughly $1.6 million in damages over the three years.

Recommendation

The company should focus first on the on-time delivery problem, since it affects every customer and threatens contract renewals. Because the data points to facility detention rather than driver capacity, the most effective first step is to audit wait times at the worst-performing facilities and renegotiate detention terms with those customers.

Underperforming routes should be repriced using the lane profitability analysis, and a driver coaching program should be built around the habits of the top performers to lift fuel efficiency and on-time rates. Finally, the company should establish a safety training program to reduce the preventable incidents that are costing over a million dollars in damages.

E-Commerce Performance Analysis using SQL and Power BI

An end-to-end analysis of an online retail operation — cleaned and calculated in SQL, then visualized in Power BI

Interact with the dashboard: View on Power BI

Performance dashboard overview showing key e-commerce metrics

Introduction

This study analyzes a dataset of 1,200 e-commerce orders in order to understand how the business is performing across sales, order fulfillment, customers, and marketing. An online store generates a steady stream of transaction records, but on their own those records say very little. The goal of this project was to turn that raw order log into a clear picture of what is working in the business and, more importantly, what is not.

The dataset is a single table of online orders, with each row representing one order placed on the store. It captures the full lifecycle of a transaction including order ID, customer ID, order date, product, quantity, unit price, total price, shipping address, payment method, order status, tracking number, cart items, referral source, and coupon code. In total the dataset spans orders placed between January 2023 and June 2025.

Data Cleaning and Preparation

For this project, the data was cleaned and prepared using SQL before being brought into Power BI for visualization. Doing the cleaning and the calculations in SQL meant the numbers were verified at the source, so that Power BI only had to focus on presenting them. The cleaning involved several steps:

1. Removed duplicate records. The order table was checked for repeated rows, and the order identifier was checked to confirm that every order appeared only once. Removing duplicates is important because a duplicated order would be counted twice and would quietly inflate both the order count and the total revenue.

2. Fixed data types and formats. Each column was reviewed to make sure it was stored in the correct form, so that dates were treated as proper dates, the quantity and item counts were treated as whole numbers, and the unit price and total price were treated as decimal values. Correct formatting matters because it determines whether dates sort properly and whether the revenue calculations return accurate results.

3. Standardized text values. Fields such as the product name, payment method, order status, and referral source were cleaned of extra spaces and made consistent in their capitalization, so that the same value would not appear in two slightly different forms and be counted as two separate categories.

4. Handled the coupon code field. Around a quarter of all orders had no coupon code at all, because those customers simply did not use a promotion. Since the field was largely empty and did not contribute meaningfully to the analysis, the coupon code column was removed so that the focus could stay on the fields that actually drive insight.

5. Calculated core measures in SQL. With the data cleaned, SQL was used to calculate the core business measures directly in the database. Total revenue was calculated, along with the average order value, the number of unique customers, the revenue generated by each product, and the totals for each year. Calculating these figures in SQL first meant they could be checked and trusted before they ever reached the dashboard.

Data Visualization

The performance dashboard was designed to give an overview of the whole business at a glance and then let the user filter into the details. Each visual was chosen for the kind of question it answers best.

KPI cards (Total Revenue, Total Orders, Total Customers, Average Order Value). Cards are the fastest way to communicate a single important number, and together these four immediately establish the size of the business before the user examines any chart. Placing total customers directly beside total orders was a deliberate choice, because seeing that the two numbers are almost identical instantly raises an important question about whether customers are returning.

Total Orders by Order Status — column chart. A column chart compares categories side by side, which makes it easy to see how orders are distributed across delivered, shipped, pending, cancelled, and returned. This visual turned out to be the most revealing on the page, because it exposes how few orders actually reach the customer.

Total Revenue by Product — horizontal bar chart. A horizontal bar chart ranks the products clearly and leaves room for the product names to remain readable, so it is easy to see which products contribute the most and how close they are to one another.

Total Orders by Referral Source — column chart. A column chart makes it simple to compare the marketing channels against each other and to see whether any single channel dominates or whether traffic is spread evenly across them.

Total Revenue by Year — line chart. A line chart is the clearest way to display change over time, and plotting revenue by year immediately shows the direction the business is heading. It is worth noting that the data for 2025 only covers the first half of the year, so that final point represents a partial year rather than a full one.

Slicers (Payment Method, Quarter, Month). Slicers were placed at the top of the dashboard so that the entire page can be filtered by how customers paid and by time period. Selecting any of these updates every visual on the page at once, allowing the user to explore the data from different angles.

Insights

1. Order fulfillment is the most serious problem. Only about 19% of orders were actually delivered, while the remaining orders were spread across pending, shipped, cancelled, and returned. With roughly a fifth of orders ending in cancellation and another fifth in returns, far more orders fail to complete than succeed, which is well below what a healthy online store should expect.

2. There is an almost complete absence of repeat customers. There were 1,189 unique customers across 1,200 orders, which means that on average each customer placed only a single order and almost never returned. This points to a customer retention problem rather than simply a problem of attracting new buyers.

3. Revenue is trending downward. Revenue fell from about $0.55 million in 2023 to roughly $0.48 million in 2024. The figure for 2025 is lower again, but because that year only includes the first six months of data, it cannot be compared directly to the full years before it. Even so, the year-over-year decline from 2023 to 2024 is real and worth attention.

4. Revenue and marketing are balanced across categories. Revenue is fairly balanced rather than concentrated. The leading products, including the chair and the printer, each generated around $196,000, with the remaining products following closely behind. Marketing performance is similarly even, with channels such as Instagram and email leading but no single referral source standing out dramatically from the rest.

Recommendation

The business should focus first on its fulfillment problem, because driving more sales will not help a store that cannot deliver the orders it already has. Understanding why so many orders are cancelled or returned, whether the cause is stock issues, slow shipping, or mismatched customer expectations, should be the immediate priority.

Alongside this, the business should build a customer retention strategy, since converting even a small share of one-time buyers into repeat customers would have a major effect on revenue given how many one-time customers already exist. The high return rate in particular deserves investigation, as returns are costly and often signal a gap between what customers expect and what they receive.
